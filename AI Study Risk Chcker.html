The Big Picture

✅ Good AI Use
→ Ask AI to explain concepts step by step
→ Generate practice questions to test yourself
→ Get feedback on a draft you already wrote
→ Brainstorm examples and counterarguments
→ Create study plans and flashcards
→ Compare viewpoints, then read real sources
❌ Risky AI Use
→ Writing full assignments for submission
→ Replacing reading with summaries only
→ Copying answers you can't explain
→ Trusting citations without verifying them
→ Using AI before making any effort yourself
→ Pasting private data into public AI tools

The Research Behind This

Sources informing this tool

AI Improves Academic Performance — When Used Well

A 2025 meta-analysis found a significant positive overall effect of AI tools on students' academic performance, particularly when used for structured learning support rather than answer generation.

Meta-analysis on AI in Education, 2025

High Dependence Linked to Lower Critical Thinking

Multiple studies found that greater AI dependence was associated with lower critical thinking scores. Students who outsourced reasoning tasks showed reduced ability to solve novel problems independently.

Educational Psychology Research, 2024–2025

40% of Teens Use AI for Schoolwork

A 2024 Common Sense Media survey found that 40% of teens had used generative AI for schoolwork. Of those, 46% said their most recent use was without teacher permission.

Common Sense Media, 2024

Only Half Verify AI Facts

Among teens who used AI for school assignments, 39% noticed a problem or inaccuracy in AI output — but only 49% said they had checked other sources to verify accuracy.

Common Sense Media, 2024

AI Detection Tools Are Not Always Fair

20% of Black teens, 10% of Latino teens, and 7% of White teens reported having schoolwork incorrectly flagged as AI-generated when it was not. AI detection is not reliable and can harm students unfairly.

Common Sense Media, 2024

UNESCO & OECD: Human-Centered AI Use

UNESCO's guidance and AI competency frameworks emphasize safe, ethical, responsible, human-centered use. The goal is AI that augments human capability, not replaces it.

UNESCO AI Competency Framework, 2024

Hallucination, Privacy & Bias Risks

A Springer systematic review highlights hallucination (AI confidently giving wrong answers), bias in training data, privacy risks from data sharing, and transparency concerns as major documented risks of over-reliance in educational contexts.

Springer Systematic Review on AI in Education, 2024

For Teachers & Parents

How to use this tool in your context

What this tool does

This interactive experience helps students self-reflect on their AI habits through realistic decision-making scenarios. It does not accuse or punish — it reveals patterns and teaches through consequences.

How to use it in class

Assign it as a 5-minute warm-up before a unit on academic integrity. Then use the results screen as a discussion prompt: "What habits showed up for you? What would you change?" The reflection is more valuable than the score.

The tool does NOT flag students

This is not a surveillance or detection tool. Results are private to the student. The goal is self-awareness and skill-building, not policing. AI detection tools have documented accuracy and fairness problems.

Key conversation starters

"Could you defend every sentence in your assignment?" / "What would you do if AI gave you a wrong answer?" / "What does using AI as a tutor vs. a shortcut look like to you?"

The nuance that matters

Research shows AI can genuinely improve academic performance when used well. The goal of this tool is not to ban AI — it's to teach the difference between AI as a learning scaffold and AI as a replacement for thinking.
